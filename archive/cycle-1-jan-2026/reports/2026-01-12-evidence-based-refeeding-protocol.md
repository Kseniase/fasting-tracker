# Evidence-Based Refeeding Protocol After Extended Water Fasting: A Scientific Review

**Date:** January 12, 2026
**Author:** Research compilation for 10-11 day water fast recovery

---

## Abstract

Refeeding after an extended water fast (10-11 days) requires careful nutritional planning to prevent refeeding syndrome, restore digestive function, and optimize metabolic recovery. This report synthesizes clinical guidelines from eating disorder treatment protocols, intensive care medicine, and sports nutrition research to justify a structured 7-10 day refeeding protocol. The evidence supports a phased approach: initial refeeding with easily digestible proteins and broths (Days 1-2, 500-800 kcal), progressive protein-focused nutrition (Days 3-5, 1200-1500 kcal with 120-130g protein), gradual carbohydrate reintroduction (Days 6-10, 1800-2200 kcal), followed by normalization to maintenance intake. Key findings emphasize the importance of phosphate, thiamine, and electrolyte monitoring; the superiority of bone broth and soft proteins for initial refeeding; the necessity of avoiding refined carbohydrates in early phases; and the role of digestive enzyme support and probiotics in gut restoration.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

Extended water fasting (defined as fasting exceeding 72 hours) induces profound metabolic adaptations. After 10-11 days of complete caloric restriction, the body operates primarily on ketone bodies derived from adipose tissue, with significant downregulation of insulin secretion, gut enzyme production, and intestinal villi activity. The transition back to normal eating represents a critical period where improper refeeding can lead to serious complications, including the potentially fatal refeeding syndrome.

### 1.2 Scope of This Review

This review examines the scientific rationale for a structured refeeding protocol consisting of:

1. **Days 1-2:** Bone broth and soft eggs (500-800 calories)
2. **Days 3-5:** Protein-focused nutrition, 120-130g/day (1200-1500 calories)
3. **Days 6-10:** Carbohydrate reintroduction (1800-2200 calories)
4. **Week 2+:** Normalization to maintenance calories

The evidence base draws from three primary domains: eating disorder treatment (which has the most extensive clinical research on refeeding), intensive care medicine (concerning refeeding syndrome prevention), and sports nutrition (regarding muscle protein synthesis optimization).

## 2. The Pathophysiology of Refeeding Syndrome

### 2.1 Definition and Mechanism

Refeeding syndrome is a potentially fatal condition characterized by severe electrolyte and fluid shifts associated with metabolic abnormalities in malnourished patients undergoing refeeding. The hallmark is hypophosphatemia, but the syndrome also involves hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, sodium retention, and thiamine deficiency.

During prolonged fasting, the body maintains serum electrolyte levels by drawing from intracellular stores and bone. Serum levels may appear normal despite total body depletion. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, insulin secretion surges dramatically (especially after a period of profound insulin suppression), driving glucose, phosphate, potassium, magnesium, and water into cells. This sudden intracellular shift depletes already marginal serum levels, potentially causing:

- Cardiac arrhythmias and heart failure
- Respiratory failure
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Seizures
- Hemolysis
- Death

### 2.2 Risk Factors After Extended Fasting

The NICE (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) guidelines identify individuals at high risk for refeeding syndrome based on specific criteria. A person who has undergone a 10-11 day water fast meets multiple criteria:

**One or more of the following:**
- BMI less than 16 kg/m2 (possible after extended fast)
- Unintentional weight loss greater than 15% in 3-6 months (typical)
- Little or no nutritional intake for more than 10 days (definitional)
- Low levels of potassium, phosphate, or magnesium before feeding (likely)

**Or two or more of the following:**
- BMI less than 18.5 kg/m2
- Unintentional weight loss greater than 10% in 3-6 months
- Little or no nutritional intake for more than 5 days
- History of alcohol misuse or certain medications

A healthy individual completing an intentional 10-11 day fast with adequate hydration and electrolyte supplementation during the fast is at moderate risk, significantly lower than a cachectic anorexia patient but still requiring careful refeeding protocols.

### 2.3 Clinical Guidelines for Calorie Introduction

The NICE guidelines recommend starting refeeding at 10 kcal/kg/day in patients at high risk, with slow increases over 4-7 days. The ASPEN (American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition) guidelines similarly recommend cautious initiation.

For a planned fast in a previously healthy individual with maintained electrolyte supplementation:
- **Days 1-2:** 5-10 kcal/kg/day (350-700 kcal for 70 kg individual)
- **Days 3-4:** 15-20 kcal/kg/day (1050-1400 kcal)
- **Days 5-7:** 20-30 kcal/kg/day (1400-2100 kcal)
- **Week 2:** Progress to full requirements

This aligns closely with the proposed protocol of 500-800 kcal (Days 1-2), 1200-1500 kcal (Days 3-5), and 1800-2200 kcal (Days 6-10).

## 3. Phase 1: Initial Refeeding (Days 1-2)

### 3.1 Why Bone Broth?

Bone broth is recommended as the first food after extended fasting for multiple evidence-based reasons:

**3.1.1 Collagen and Gut Integrity**

Extended fasting causes intestinal mucosal atrophy. The gut epithelium has the highest turnover rate of any tissue in the body (complete renewal every 3-5 days), and without luminal nutrients, the villi shorten and absorptive capacity diminishes. Bone broth provides:

- **Glycine and glutamine:** The two most abundant amino acids in bone broth are critical for enterocyte function. Glutamine is the primary fuel source for intestinal epithelial cells (not glucose). Research from the Journal of Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition demonstrates that glutamine supplementation improves intestinal barrier function and reduces bacterial translocation in fasted subjects.

- **Collagen peptides:** Hydrolyzed collagen provides building blocks for tight junction repair. Studies in the journal Gut show that collagen-derived peptides support mucosal healing.

- **Gelatin:** Creates a soothing coating on the intestinal lining, reducing inflammation and supporting mucus production.

**3.1.2 Electrolyte Content**

Properly prepared bone broth (simmered 24-48 hours with added apple cider vinegar to leach minerals) provides:
- Calcium: 30-50 mg per cup
- Magnesium: 5-10 mg per cup
- Phosphorus: 50-70 mg per cup
- Potassium: 100-200 mg per cup
- Sodium: 300-500 mg per cup (varies with seasoning)

This mineral content supports gradual electrolyte repletion without the rapid shifts caused by oral electrolyte supplements.

**3.1.3 Minimal Insulin Stimulation**

Bone broth is virtually carbohydrate-free (typically less than 1g per cup). This is crucial because:
- Insulin secretion remains low, preventing rapid intracellular electrolyte shifts
- Ketosis is maintained initially, allowing gradual metabolic transition
- Blood glucose remains stable, avoiding reactive hypoglycemia

**3.1.4 High Bioavailability**

The liquid form requires minimal digestive effort. After 10+ days of fasting, pancreatic enzyme production and gastric acid secretion are significantly reduced. Bone broth's pre-digested amino acids are absorbed with minimal digestive work.

### 3.2 Why Soft Eggs?

Eggs are introduced on Day 1-2 for specific nutritional and practical reasons:

**3.2.1 Complete Protein Profile**

Eggs provide all essential amino acids in near-optimal ratios. The Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score (PDCAAS) of eggs is 1.0 (the highest possible), meaning 100% of the protein is bioavailable.

**3.2.2 Phospholipids and Choline**

Egg yolks are rich in phosphatidylcholine, which supports:
- Bile production (essential for fat digestion resumption)
- Cell membrane repair
- Neurotransmitter synthesis (acetylcholine)

**3.2.3 Preparation Method Matters**

"Soft eggs" specifically refers to soft-boiled, poached, or gently scrambled eggs. This preparation:
- Denatures proteins for easier digestion
- Preserves heat-sensitive nutrients
- Avoids added fats that stress the gallbladder
- Creates a soft texture requiring minimal mechanical digestion

Hard-boiled eggs, while acceptable, have slightly reduced digestibility due to protein cross-linking from extended cooking.

**3.2.4 Fat Content Considerations**

The fat in eggs (approximately 5g per egg) provides essential fatty acids and fat-soluble vitamin transport. However, after extended fasting, bile secretion is reduced. Starting with 2-3 eggs per day prevents overwhelming the hepatobiliary system while providing adequate nutrition.

### 3.3 Caloric Target Justification: 500-800 kcal

The 500-800 calorie range for Days 1-2 is supported by:

- NICE guidelines: 10 kcal/kg/day for high-risk patients (700 kcal for 70 kg)
- Clinical experience from eating disorder treatment: Starting at 500-600 kcal minimizes refeeding complications
- Physiological tolerance: The atrophied gut can process this volume without distress
- Metabolic continuity: Low caloric intake maintains partial ketosis, allowing gradual transition

## 4. Phase 2: Protein-Focused Nutrition (Days 3-5)

### 4.1 Optimal Protein Intake for Muscle Rebuilding

Extended fasting inevitably causes some lean mass loss despite the body's protein-sparing mechanisms during ketosis. Research from the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition indicates that muscle protein synthesis (MPS) is suppressed during prolonged fasting but can be rapidly restored with adequate protein intake.

**4.1.1 The 120-130g/day Target**

This protein intake is based on:

- **Body weight calculations:** 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight is the evidence-based range for maximizing muscle protein synthesis during recomposition phases. For an individual weighing 65-70 kg post-fast, this equals 104-154 g/day.

- **Leucine threshold research:** Studies by Layman et al. demonstrate that 2.5-3g of leucine per meal is required to maximally stimulate MPS. This requires approximately 25-40g of high-quality protein per meal. Four protein-containing meals at 30-35g each reaches the target.

- **Anabolic resistance consideration:** After prolonged catabolism, muscle tissue may exhibit transient anabolic resistance. Higher protein intake helps overcome this threshold.

**4.1.2 Protein Timing and Distribution**

Research from Phillips and colleagues shows that even distribution of protein throughout the day (every 3-5 hours) optimizes 24-hour muscle protein synthesis compared to skewed distribution. The protocol should include:
- 4 protein-rich meals
- 25-35g protein per meal
- 3-4 hour intervals between meals

**4.1.3 Protein Sources During This Phase**

Recommended proteins for Days 3-5 include:
- Eggs (continued)
- Fish (especially fatty fish like salmon for omega-3s)
- Poultry (chicken, turkey)
- Greek yogurt (if dairy tolerated)
- Cottage cheese
- Tofu/tempeh (if plant-based)

Red meat is typically introduced later (Day 5-6) due to:
- Higher fat content requiring robust bile production
- Longer gastric emptying time
- Greater digestive enzyme requirements

### 4.2 Why Limit Carbohydrates in Days 3-5?

Carbohydrates remain limited (under 50-75g/day) during this phase for several reasons:

**4.2.1 Insulin Sensitivity**

Extended fasting dramatically increases peripheral insulin sensitivity. Research from Cell Metabolism shows that fasting upregulates GLUT4 transporters and enhances insulin receptor sensitivity. Introducing large carbohydrate loads to this sensitized system causes:
- Exaggerated insulin spikes
- Rapid hypoglycemia
- Severe electrolyte shifts
- Subjective symptoms (shakiness, sweating, confusion)

**4.2.2 Maintaining Metabolic Flexibility**

The body has adapted to efficient fat oxidation. Gradual carbohydrate reintroduction allows:
- Enzymatic upregulation of glycolytic pathways
- Gradual restoration of glycogen synthase activity
- Prevention of rapid fat regain due to insulin-mediated lipogenesis

**4.2.3 Continued Refeeding Syndrome Prevention**

The primary driver of refeeding syndrome is carbohydrate-induced insulin secretion. Keeping carbohydrates low while increasing protein and fat allows caloric progression with minimal risk.

### 4.3 Caloric Target Justification: 1200-1500 kcal

The increase to 1200-1500 calories aligns with:
- NICE progression: 15-20 kcal/kg/day by Days 3-5
- Clinical protocols: 50% of estimated needs by Day 4-5
- Tolerance threshold: The gut has begun regenerating and can process increased volume

## 5. Phase 3: Carbohydrate Reintroduction (Days 6-10)

### 5.1 Timing of Carbohydrate Introduction

By Day 6, several adaptations make carbohydrate reintroduction safe:

- **Electrolyte stabilization:** Serum phosphate, potassium, and magnesium have normalized with supplementation and dietary intake
- **Insulin responsiveness modulation:** The system has begun re-adapting to dietary insulin stimulation
- **Digestive capacity restoration:** Pancreatic enzyme production has increased; brush border enzymes are regenerating
- **Gut microbiome transition:** Fiber-fermenting bacteria are ready for substrate

### 5.2 Types of Carbohydrates to Introduce

**5.2.1 Recommended: Complex, Low-Glycemic Carbohydrates**

- **Non-starchy vegetables:** Introduce from Day 1 in small amounts (broccoli, spinach, zucchini)
- **Berries:** Lower glycemic index fruit, rich in polyphenols
- **Sweet potatoes:** Complex starches, high in potassium
- **Legumes:** Protein-containing carbohydrates with fiber
- **Whole grains:** Oats, quinoa, brown rice (Day 7+)

**5.2.2 Foods to Delay Until Week 2**

- White rice and bread (rapid glucose spike)
- Tropical fruits (high glycemic load)
- Fruit juices (liquid sugar without fiber)
- Processed foods (inflammatory, low nutrient density)
- Alcohol (hepatotoxic during recovery, thiamine-depleting)

### 5.3 The Glycogen Replenishment Process

Muscle and liver glycogen stores are depleted after extended fasting. Full replenishment requires:

- **Total glycogen capacity:** Approximately 400-500g (muscle) + 100g (liver)
- **Replenishment rate:** Maximum 5-7% of depleted stores per hour with optimal carbohydrate intake
- **Practical timeline:** Full replenishment over 3-5 days of adequate carbohydrate intake

The 1800-2200 calorie range with increasing carbohydrate proportion (40-50% of calories by Day 10) supports this replenishment while avoiding excessive insulin stimulation.

## 6. Critical Supplementation Considerations

### 6.1 Thiamine (Vitamin B1)

Thiamine is arguably the most critical micronutrient during refeeding:

- **Mechanism:** Thiamine is a cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase, transketolase, and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase. Without adequate thiamine, carbohydrate metabolism produces toxic intermediates.
- **Deficiency consequences:** Wernicke's encephalopathy, Korsakoff syndrome, cardiac beriberi
- **Recommendation:** NICE guidelines mandate thiamine supplementation before initiating refeeding in at-risk patients (200-300 mg oral thiamine daily, or IV if severe)
- **Timeline:** Begin 30 minutes before first caloric intake; continue for 10+ days

### 6.2 Phosphate

- **Monitoring:** Serum phosphate should be checked before refeeding and daily during the first week if possible
- **Supplementation:** If serum phosphate < 0.5 mmol/L, IV replacement may be necessary; oral supplementation (phosphate-sandoz or equivalent) for moderate depletion
- **Food sources:** Eggs, fish, dairy, meat contain phosphate for gradual replacement

### 6.3 Potassium

- **Target:** Maintain serum potassium 3.5-5.0 mmol/L
- **Supplementation:** Potassium chloride supplements (20-40 mEq/day) if needed
- **Food sources:** Avocado, salmon, sweet potato, spinach

### 6.4 Magnesium

- **Target:** Maintain serum magnesium > 0.7 mmol/L
- **Supplementation:** Magnesium glycinate (400-600 mg/day) for best absorption and tolerance
- **Note:** Serum magnesium poorly reflects total body stores; err on the side of supplementation

### 6.5 Sodium

Paradoxically, sodium restriction is often appropriate during early refeeding:
- Refeeding causes sodium and water retention
- Edema is common in early refeeding
- Let the body regulate sodium naturally through food content

## 7. Digestive System Restoration

### 7.1 Enzyme Considerations

After extended fasting, digestive enzyme production is suppressed:

**7.1.1 Pancreatic Enzymes**
- Lipase, protease, and amylase secretion decreases during fasting
- Full restoration takes 7-10 days of eating
- Consider digestive enzyme supplementation with meals during Days 1-7

**7.1.2 Brush Border Enzymes**
- Lactase, maltase, and sucrase are produced by intestinal cells
- These enzymes require dietary substrate for upregulation
- Temporary lactose intolerance is common; avoid dairy or use lactase supplements initially

**7.1.3 Gastric Acid**
- HCl production is reduced after fasting
- Betaine HCl supplementation may aid protein digestion
- Avoid antacids during refeeding

### 7.2 Gut Microbiome Restoration

The gut microbiome undergoes significant shifts during extended fasting:

**7.2.1 Fasting Changes**
- Reduced bacterial diversity
- Shift toward mucin-degrading bacteria (Akkermansia)
- Reduced Firmicutes (fiber-fermenting bacteria)

**7.2.2 Restoration Protocol**
- **Probiotics:** Introduce multi-strain probiotic (10-50 billion CFU) from Day 3
- **Prebiotics:** Gradually increase fiber intake (10g Day 3-5, 20g Day 6-10)
- **Fermented foods:** Introduce by Day 5-7 (sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir, yogurt)

### 7.3 Foods to Absolutely Avoid in First 48 Hours

Based on clinical experience and physiological principles:

1. **High-fiber raw vegetables:** Physically abrasive to atrophied mucosa
2. **Nuts and seeds:** Require extensive mastication and enzymatic breakdown
3. **Fried foods:** Overwhelm bile secretion capacity
4. **Sugar/refined carbohydrates:** Trigger refeeding syndrome, reactive hypoglycemia
5. **Alcohol:** Hepatotoxic, depletes thiamine
6. **Caffeine:** Stimulates gastric acid on empty stomach, diuretic
7. **Spicy foods:** Irritating to sensitive mucosa
8. **Dairy (for many):** Lactase insufficiency common
9. **Red meat:** Too complex for initial digestion
10. **Large meals:** Physical distension painful; small frequent meals preferred

## 8. Special Considerations for Intentional Fasting vs. Starvation

It is important to note that most clinical refeeding research derives from:
- Eating disorder patients (often severely malnourished over months/years)
- Famine victims (chronically malnourished, often with infections)
- Post-surgical patients (often compromised metabolically)

An individual completing a planned 10-11 day water fast with:
- Adequate starting weight/muscle mass
- Proper electrolyte supplementation during fast
- No underlying metabolic disease
- Medical monitoring available

...has a significantly lower risk profile than these clinical populations. The recommendations in this document represent a conservative, evidence-based approach that errs on the side of caution.

For such individuals, some clinicians might advocate slightly faster caloric progression. However, given the irreversibility of refeeding syndrome complications, the conservative approach is warranted.

## 10. Conclusion

The evidence strongly supports a phased, cautious approach to refeeding after extended water fasting. The proposed protocol of bone broth and soft eggs (Days 1-2), protein-focused nutrition (Days 3-5), and gradual carbohydrate reintroduction (Days 6-10) aligns with clinical guidelines from eating disorder treatment, intensive care medicine, and sports nutrition research.

Key principles include:
1. **Start low, go slow:** Begin at 10 kcal/kg/day, increase by 5-10 kcal/kg every 2-3 days
2. **Protein first:** Prioritize complete proteins for gut and muscle restoration
3. **Delay carbohydrates:** Avoid significant carbohydrate intake until Day 5-6
4. **Supplement critical nutrients:** Thiamine, phosphate, potassium, magnesium
5. **Support digestion:** Digestive enzymes, probiotics, small frequent meals
6. **Monitor for complications:** Edema, rapid heart rate, confusion warrant medical attention

The 7-10 day refeeding period represents a critical investment in long-term health outcomes. Rushing this process risks refeeding syndrome, rapid weight regain, and digestive distress. Patience during refeeding maximizes the metabolic benefits achieved during the fast itself.
