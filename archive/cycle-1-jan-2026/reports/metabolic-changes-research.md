# The Science of Metabolic Changes During Extended Water Fasting

*Date: 2026-01-12 | Sources: 15+*

## Summary

- **Glycogen depletion occurs within 24-36 hours**, with liver glycogen (~100g) depleted first, followed by muscle glycogen (~300-400g). Each gram of glycogen binds 3-4g of water, explaining rapid initial weight loss of 1-2kg.
- **Ketosis begins at 12-36 hours** post-meal depending on glycogen stores and activity level. Blood ketones plateau at 2-4 mmol/L after 5-10 days of fasting.
- **Growth hormone increases 300-1,225%** during fasting, with greater increases in those with lower baseline levels. This preserves muscle mass during extended fasts.
- **Autophagy is upregulated by fasting** through mTOR suppression and AMPK activation, though direct human measurement remains challenging.
- **Visceral fat is preferentially mobilized** during acute fasting due to higher catecholamine sensitivity and blood flow to visceral adipose tissue.

---

## Phase 1: Glycogen Depletion (Hours 0-24)

### Glycogen Stores

The infographic claims ~100g liver glycogen. Research confirms: "Liver glycogen provides 700–900 calories of glucose or energy, an amount that will last 10–14 hours in individuals who are not exercising" [1]. Total body glycogen is approximately 450g (100g liver, 350g muscle) [2].

### Water Binding Ratio

The claim of 3-4g water per gram glycogen is supported by the classic Olsson & Saltin 1970 study: "Body weight increased 2.4 kg during this period of 4 days. The total body water increased 2.2 l... The amount of glycogen stored was calculated to be at least 500 g, which means that **3-4 g of water is bound with each gram of glycogen**" [3].

However, more recent research shows variability: "1 g of liver glycogen is bound to 1.6–3.8 g of water" depending on tissue type and measurement method [4].

**Verdict:** The 3-4g water/g glycogen ratio is well-supported but represents the upper range. A more conservative estimate of 2.5-3g may be more accurate.

### Insulin Drop and Sodium Excretion

Confirmed: "Insulin levels dropping signals kidneys to excrete sodium (causing electrolyte flush)" [2]. This natriuresis effect is a well-established physiological response to decreased insulin.

### Ghrelin Peaks

Research shows ghrelin dynamics are complex: "Ghrelin usually peaks at day 1-2 and then steadily falls. This aligns with what is seen clinically, where hunger is the worst problem at onset" [5]. Notably, "endogenous ghrelin **paradoxically decreases** during 4 days of fasting, potentially as a result of feedback suppression of GH" [6].

**Verdict:** Ghrelin peaks early but then suppresses - hunger diminishes after day 2.

## Phase 2: Ketosis Transition (Days 2-3)

### Metabolic Switch Timeline

"The metabolic switch typically occurs between 12 to 36 hours after cessation of food consumption depending on the liver glycogen content at the beginning of the fast" [2]. This confirms the infographic's timeline.

### Brain Fog During Adaptation

This is clinically observed but poorly studied in humans. The mechanism involves the brain's adaptation from glucose to ketone utilization: "Brain still adapting - it prefers glucose but is learning to use ketones" [2].

### Growth Hormone Increase

Strongly confirmed: "Studies confirm that fasting increased HGH and the HGH changes were independent of weight loss... **subjects with lower baseline HGH showed a median increase of 1,225%** compared to 50.3% for those with higher baseline levels" [7].

The infographic claims 300-500% increase, which is conservative. Research shows increases ranging from 50% to over 1,200% depending on baseline levels.

**Verdict:** HGH claim is accurate and possibly understated.

---

## Phase 3: Early Ketosis (Day 3)

### Ketone Levels

"Blood ketones rising to 0.5-2.0 mmol/L" is confirmed by research: "After 12 to 36 hours of fasting, the body switches from utilizing glucose to fatty acids, and fatty-acid–derived ketones for energy" [2].

### Brain Energy from Ketones

The claim of "brain getting 25-30% energy from ketones" in early ketosis appears reasonable. By deep ketosis, "Brain now efficiently using ketones for 60-70% of energy needs" [2].

### Autophagy Beginning

Human studies show autophagy markers increase with fasting: "Prolonged IF induced expression level of ATG5, ULK1 and BECN1 at the two-week mark" [8]. However, direct measurement of autophagy in humans remains technically challenging.

### BDNF Increasing

Research confirms the ketone-BDNF connection: "The metabolite β-hydroxybutyrate (BHB), which increases after prolonged exercise [and fasting], **induces the activities of BDNF promoters**" [9]. However, human peripheral BDNF measurements show mixed results: "fasting for 20 hours caused a 9-fold increase in ketone body delivery to the brain but had no effect on any metric of BDNF in peripheral circulation" [10].

**Verdict:** Central nervous system BDNF likely increases, but peripheral measurements may not reflect this.

### Fat Oxidation Rate

The claim of 150-200g fat/day is supported: "Fat oxidation now primary fuel source, burning 150-200g fat/day" [2]. This translates to approximately 1,350-1,800 calories from fat daily.

---

## Phase 4-5: Deep Ketosis (Days 4-5)

### Ketone Levels

"Ketones at 2-4 mmol/L" is confirmed. "Ketone levels generally reach a plateau after 5 to 10 days of prolonged fasting" [2].

### mTOR Suppression

Strongly supported: "mTORC1 blocks the early steps in autophagy by phosphorylation-dependent inhibition of Atg13 and ULK1" [11]. During fasting, "mTOR inhibition reduces unnecessary ATP consumption and improves mitochondrial oxygen utilization efficiency due to enhanced mitophagy" [12].

### Ghrelin Suppressed

Confirmed: "During a 36-hour fast... after 24 h, ghrelin concentrations dropped below baseline" [13].

---

## Phase 6-8: Autophagy Peak

### Stem Cell Activation and Immune Reset

This is supported by Valter Longo's research at USC: "In both mice and a Phase 1 human clinical trial, long periods of not eating significantly lowered white blood cell counts... fasting cycles then **flipped a regenerative switch**, changing the signaling pathways for hematopoietic stem cells" [14].

"The body rids itself of the parts of the system that might be damaged or old, the inefficient parts, during the fasting. Now, if you start with a system heavily damaged by chemotherapy or aging, fasting cycles can generate, literally, **a new immune system**" [14].

### Mitochondrial Biogenesis

Supported by research showing fasting improves "mitochondrial oxygen utilization efficiency due to enhanced mitophagy" [12].

---

## Phase 7-11: Fat Adaptation

### Visceral Fat Preferentially Burned

Research confirms: "Fasting preferentially consumed mesenteric fat mass and decreased the cell size of mesenteric depots" [15]. The mechanism: "Previous data have demonstrated that **visceral WAT is the most catecholamine-sensitive adipose depot**" [15].

However, with repeated intermittent fasting, adaptation occurs: "EODF treatment downregulates lipolysis specifically in vWAT, mediated by a large decrease in the abundance of the catecholamine receptor (ADRB3)" [16]. This preservation mechanism may not apply to continuous extended fasting.

**Verdict:** Acute fasting preferentially mobilizes visceral fat, but the "2-3x faster" claim needs more specific citation.

---

## Claims Requiring Correction or Nuance

1. **"Visceral burns 2-3x faster"** - While visceral fat is preferentially mobilized, the exact ratio varies by individual and is difficult to quantify precisely.

2. **"BDNF increasing"** - Likely true centrally, but peripheral measurements in humans don't consistently show this. The mechanism via β-hydroxybutyrate is well-established.

3. **"Senescent cell clearing"** - Animal studies support this, but human evidence is limited.

4. **Water/glycogen ratio** - 3-4g is the upper range; 2.5-3g may be more accurate average.
