# The Mental and Cognitive Journey During Extended Water Fasting: A Scientific Review

**Date:** January 12, 2026
**Author:** Research Report
**Subject:** Neurological and Psychological Effects of Extended Water Fasting and Ketosis

---

## Abstract

Extended water fasting induces a profound metabolic shift from glucose to ketone body utilization, with significant implications for brain function and cognition. This review examines the scientific evidence underlying the characteristic mental and cognitive changes experienced during multi-day fasts: the initial "brain fog" phase during glucose depletion, the transitional period of keto-adaptation, the emergence of mental clarity as ketone utilization increases, and the sustained cognitive benefits reported during extended fasting. We evaluate the neurobiological mechanisms including ketone body metabolism in the brain, norepinephrine elevation, brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) upregulation, and the neuroprotective properties of beta-hydroxybutyrate. The evidence reveals a consistent pattern wherein initial cognitive impairment gives way to enhanced mental clarity, though the magnitude of subjective experiences like "fasting euphoria" may exceed what controlled studies demonstrate. This review distinguishes between well-established mechanisms and areas requiring further research.

---

## 1. Introduction

The human brain, comprising approximately 2% of body weight, consumes roughly 20% of the body's total energy expenditure. Under normal fed conditions, glucose serves as the brain's primary fuel source, with the brain consuming approximately 120-130 grams of glucose daily (Mergenthaler et al., 2013). However, during extended fasting, the body's limited glycogen stores become depleted within 24-48 hours, necessitating alternative fuel sources for neural tissue that cannot directly oxidize fatty acids.

This metabolic transition from glucose to ketone body utilization represents one of the most significant adaptive mechanisms in human physiology. The cognitive and psychological experiences during this transition follow a predictable pattern that aligns with underlying neurochemical changes. Understanding these mechanisms provides insight into both the challenges of early fasting and the cognitive benefits reported during sustained ketosis.

This review examines the scientific literature supporting the mental and cognitive journey during extended water fasting, evaluating claims ranging from initial brain fog to reported euphoria, and distinguishing between robustly supported mechanisms and more speculative assertions.

## 2. Days 1-2: Brain Fog and Cognitive Decline During Glucose Depletion

### 2.1 The Metabolic Context

During the first 24-48 hours of fasting, the body depletes hepatic glycogen stores while fatty acid oxidation increases. Plasma glucose levels decline but remain within physiological range due to gluconeogenesis. The brain, however, experiences a relative energy deficit as glucose availability decreases before ketone production reaches sufficient levels to compensate (Cahill, 2006).

### 2.2 Cognitive Impairment During Early Fasting

Multiple studies have documented cognitive decrements during short-term fasting. A systematic review by Benau et al. (2014) examined cognitive performance during acute fasting and found that tasks requiring sustained attention, working memory, and executive function showed impairment during the first 24-48 hours of fasting in many, though not all, studies.

Green et al. (1995) demonstrated that 24-hour fasting produced measurable decrements in vigilance tasks and immediate memory, consistent with the "brain fog" reported anecdotally. Similarly, Lieberman et al. (2008) found that 24-hour fasting in military personnel resulted in slower reaction times and impaired short-term memory.

The mechanism underlying early cognitive impairment likely relates to the metabolic transition period wherein glucose availability declines before ketone production compensates. The brain requires time to upregulate the enzymatic machinery for ketone utilization, including monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1 and MCT2) that transport ketones across the blood-brain barrier (Pierre & Bhide, 2006).

### 2.3 Irritability and Mood Disturbance

The irritability commonly reported during early fasting has neurobiological correlates. Declining blood glucose activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, increasing cortisol secretion. Additionally, falling glucose levels trigger release of counter-regulatory hormones including adrenaline and noradrenaline, which can manifest as irritability, anxiety, and difficulty concentrating (Gonder-Frederick et al., 1989).

## 3. Days 2-3: The Transition Period

### 3.1 Ketogenesis and Rising Ketone Levels

By 48-72 hours of fasting, hepatic ketogenesis increases substantially. Beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) and acetoacetate, the primary circulating ketone bodies, begin to rise to levels sufficient for brain utilization. Plasma BHB concentrations typically reach 1-2 mM by day 3 of fasting, compared to less than 0.3 mM in the fed state (Cahill et al., 1966).

### 3.2 Brain Adaptation to Ketone Metabolism

The brain's adaptation to ketone utilization is not instantaneous. The process of "keto-adaptation" involves upregulation of enzymes necessary for ketone metabolism, including 3-oxoacid CoA-transferase (SCOT) and acetoacetyl-CoA thiolase, as well as increased expression of monocarboxylate transporters (Puchalska & Crawford, 2017).

During this transitional phase, the brain operates in a metabolically suboptimal state: glucose remains the preferred fuel, but availability is reduced, while ketone utilization machinery is still being upregulated. This metabolic inefficiency likely contributes to the continued brain fog and mood instability reported during days 2-3 of fasting.

### 3.3 Evidence for Mood Variability

Controlled studies of ketogenic diets, which induce ketosis without caloric restriction, provide insight into the mood effects of the metabolic transition. Yancy et al. (2004) reported that patients initiating ketogenic diets experienced transient mood disturbances during the first week, coinciding with the keto-adaptation period.

## 4. Days 3-4: Emergence of Mental Clarity

### 4.1 Ketone Bodies as Brain Fuel

By days 3-4 of fasting, ketone body concentrations reach levels (2-4 mM) that can supply a substantial portion of the brain's energy requirements. Owen et al. (1967) demonstrated in their landmark studies of prolonged fasting that ketone bodies eventually supply up to 60-70% of the brain's energy needs, sparing glucose for cells that cannot utilize ketones.

Importantly, ketone metabolism is more efficient than glucose metabolism in terms of ATP yield per molecule of oxygen consumed. BHB produces more ATP per unit oxygen than glucose, effectively providing an "octane boost" to brain tissue (Veech et al., 2001).

### 4.2 Improved Cognitive Function on Ketogenic Diets

Evidence from ketogenic diet studies supports improved cognitive function once keto-adaptation is achieved. Krikorian et al. (2012) found that older adults following a low-carbohydrate diet showed improved memory performance compared to a high-carbohydrate diet group, with improvements correlating with ketone levels.

Reger et al. (2004) demonstrated that acute elevation of plasma ketone bodies through medium-chain triglyceride administration improved cognitive function in subjects with memory impairment, directly implicating ketones in cognitive enhancement.

### 4.3 The Transition Point

Clinical observations and anecdotal reports consistently identify days 3-4 as a transition point where brain fog lifts and mental clarity begins to emerge. This timing correlates with the establishment of sufficient ketone production and the completion of initial keto-adaptation processes in the brain.

---

## 5. Days 4-5: "Keto Clarity" and Enhanced Cognitive Function

### 5.1 The Phenomenon of Enhanced Mental Clarity

The term "keto clarity" describes the subjective experience of enhanced mental focus, reduced brain fog, and improved cognitive function that many individuals report during established ketosis. While this phenomenon has been extensively documented in anecdotal accounts and popular literature, controlled studies provide partial support for these experiences.

### 5.2 Scientific Evidence for Cognitive Enhancement

Murray et al. (2016) found that dietary ketosis improved working memory, visual attention, and processing speed in healthy adults compared to a high-carbohydrate diet. Similarly, Iacovides et al. (2019) reported that keto-adapted athletes showed no cognitive decrements despite being in negative energy balance, suggesting ketones provide adequate fuel for cognitive function even during caloric restriction.

Studies in epilepsy patients, who maintain therapeutic ketosis for extended periods, demonstrate preserved or improved cognitive function on ketogenic diets (Lambrechts et al., 2013). While these findings support the basic claim of adequate cognitive function during ketosis, they offer more modest support for "enhancement" beyond baseline levels.

### 5.3 Reduced Inflammation and Cognitive Benefits

Ketone bodies, particularly BHB, have demonstrated anti-inflammatory properties that may contribute to cognitive benefits. BHB inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome, reducing production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (Youm et al., 2015). Given the role of neuroinflammation in cognitive decline and brain fog, this anti-inflammatory effect provides a mechanistic basis for improved mental clarity during ketosis.

## 6. Day 5 and Beyond: Sustained Mental Clarity and Enhanced Creativity

### 6.1 Metabolic Stability in Sustained Ketosis

By day 5 and beyond, the body has achieved metabolic stability in the fasted state. Ketone production and utilization reach equilibrium, gluconeogenesis maintains stable blood glucose, and counter-regulatory hormone responses normalize. This metabolic stability likely underlies the consistent mental state reported during extended fasting.

### 6.2 Evidence for Sustained Cognitive Function

Extended fasting studies, while limited due to ethical constraints, suggest cognitive function is maintained or improved during prolonged ketosis. In Cahill's classic studies of 40-day therapeutic fasts (Cahill et al., 1966), subjects reported subjective improvements in mental clarity after the initial adaptation period, though formal cognitive testing was limited.

### 6.3 Claims of Enhanced Creativity

The claim that fasting enhances creativity lacks robust controlled research. While numerous anecdotal reports from historical figures and contemporary practitioners describe creative insights during fasting, no controlled studies have specifically examined creative output during extended fasting.

The plausibility of enhanced creativity rests on several mechanisms:
1. Increased norepinephrine may enhance novel associations
2. Altered default mode network activity during ketosis (Mujica-Parodi et al., 2020)
3. Reduced inflammation improving brain network efficiency

However, these remain speculative explanations for anecdotal observations rather than scientifically established facts.

### 6.4 Emotional Stability

Reports of emotional stability during sustained fasting may relate to stabilized blood glucose (eliminating glucose-driven mood swings), reduced inflammation, and adaptation to the fasted hormonal milieu. However, controlled studies specifically examining emotional regulation during extended fasting are lacking.

## 7. Norepinephrine Elevation and Alertness

### 7.1 Catecholamine Response to Fasting

Fasting triggers a well-documented increase in catecholamine release, including norepinephrine (noradrenaline). Zauner et al. (2000) demonstrated that 84-hour fasting increased plasma norepinephrine by approximately 50%. This elevation serves multiple physiological purposes, including maintenance of blood pressure and stimulation of lipolysis.

### 7.2 Cognitive Effects of Elevated Norepinephrine

Norepinephrine is a key neuromodulator involved in attention, arousal, and cognitive performance. Elevated norepinephrine during fasting likely contributes to the heightened alertness and "wired" feeling reported by many fasters (Arnsten, 2011).

The locus coeruleus-norepinephrine system modulates the signal-to-noise ratio in cortical processing, enhancing focus on relevant stimuli. This mechanism provides a neurobiological basis for the enhanced focus reported during established ketosis.

### 7.3 The Double-Edged Sword

While moderate norepinephrine elevation enhances attention and cognitive performance, excessive levels can impair prefrontal cortex function and cause anxiety. The irritability and anxiety sometimes reported during fasting may relate to catecholamine elevation, particularly when combined with the metabolic stress of early fasting (Arnsten, 2009).

---

## 8. BDNF Increases During Fasting

### 8.1 BDNF and Brain Function

Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) is a member of the neurotrophin family critical for neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function. Low BDNF levels are associated with depression, cognitive impairment, and neurodegenerative diseases (Miranda et al., 2019).

### 8.2 Evidence for Fasting-Induced BDNF Increases

Mattson and colleagues have extensively studied the relationship between caloric restriction, fasting, and BDNF expression. Animal studies consistently demonstrate that intermittent fasting increases hippocampal BDNF expression (Mattson et al., 2018). Duan et al. (2001) showed that intermittent fasting increased BDNF levels in rodents and conferred neuroprotection.

In humans, the evidence is more limited but supportive. Baik et al. (2020) found that intermittent fasting increased serum BDNF levels in healthy adults. However, most human studies have examined intermittent fasting rather than extended water fasting.

### 8.3 Mechanisms of BDNF Upregulation

The mechanism linking fasting to BDNF involves several pathways:
1. Ketone bodies, particularly BHB, directly induce BDNF expression (Sleiman et al., 2016)
2. Metabolic stress activates cellular stress response pathways that upregulate neurotrophic factors
3. Reduced oxidative stress during ketosis promotes BDNF expression

### 8.4 Cognitive Implications

BDNF supports synaptic plasticity and long-term potentiation, the cellular basis of learning and memory. Elevated BDNF during fasting could theoretically enhance these processes, though demonstrating cognitive benefits attributable specifically to BDNF changes (versus other fasting-induced changes) remains challenging.

## 9. Ketones as Neuroprotective Agents

### 9.1 Direct Neuroprotective Properties of BHB

Beta-hydroxybutyrate possesses several neuroprotective properties independent of its role as an energy substrate:

1. **HDAC Inhibition**: BHB acts as an endogenous histone deacetylase inhibitor, promoting expression of neuroprotective genes including BDNF and antioxidant enzymes (Shimazu et al., 2013).

2. **Anti-inflammatory Effects**: As noted, BHB inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome, reducing neuroinflammation (Youm et al., 2015).

3. **Oxidative Stress Reduction**: Ketone metabolism produces fewer reactive oxygen species than glucose metabolism, reducing oxidative damage (Veech et al., 2001).

4. **Enhanced Mitochondrial Function**: BHB improves mitochondrial biogenesis and function, supporting neuronal energy metabolism (Bough & Rho, 2007).

### 9.2 Clinical Evidence for Neuroprotection

The ketogenic diet's efficacy in treating drug-resistant epilepsy, established since the 1920s, provides clinical evidence for ketone neuroprotection. More recent research has explored ketogenic interventions in Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, and traumatic brain injury, with promising preliminary results (Pinto et al., 2018).

### 9.3 Relevance to Extended Fasting

During extended water fasting, sustained elevation of ketone bodies (3-7 mM) exposes the brain to concentrations that would be expected to confer neuroprotective benefits based on mechanistic studies. However, whether a 10-day fast produces lasting neuroprotective effects in healthy individuals remains unestablished.

## 10. The "Euphoria" Phenomenon

### 10.1 Anecdotal Reports

Many practitioners of extended fasting report experiences variously described as euphoria, bliss, heightened awareness, or spiritual transcendence. These reports are common in religious fasting traditions and contemporary fasting communities alike.

### 10.2 Potential Mechanisms

Several neurobiological mechanisms could contribute to euphoric experiences during extended fasting:

1. **Endorphin Release**: Fasting triggers endogenous opioid release as part of the stress response. Beta-endorphin, in particular, produces analgesic and euphoric effects (Mougin et al., 1987).

2. **Norepinephrine and Dopamine**: Elevated catecholamines can produce feelings of energy and well-being, similar to stimulant effects.

3. **Ketone-Induced Effects**: Some researchers have speculated that very high ketone levels may have psychoactive properties, though this remains speculative.

4. **Altered Default Mode Network**: Preliminary research suggests ketosis alters default mode network connectivity (Mujica-Parodi et al., 2020), which could affect self-referential thinking and consciousness.

5. **Psychological Factors**: The sense of accomplishment, discipline, and spiritual practice associated with fasting may contribute to positive emotional states independent of neurochemical changes.

### 10.3 Scientific Caveats

The euphoria reported during extended fasting has not been rigorously studied in controlled research. Several important caveats apply:

1. **Selection Bias**: Those who report euphoric fasting experiences may be predisposed to such experiences, while those with negative experiences may discontinue fasting.

2. **Expectation Effects**: Knowledge of reported euphoria may create expectations that influence subjective experience.

3. **Variability**: Not all fasters report euphoria, and the experience appears highly variable between individuals.

4. **Distinction from Impairment**: Some symptoms described as euphoria could reflect mild cognitive impairment or altered consciousness rather than enhancement.

The euphoria phenomenon remains one of the more speculative aspects of fasting neuroscience, supported by plausible mechanisms but lacking controlled research specifically validating the experience.

## 11. Summary of Evidence Quality

### 11.1 Well-Established Mechanisms (Strong Scientific Support)

- Brain utilization of ketone bodies during fasting
- Initial cognitive impairment during glucose depletion
- Norepinephrine elevation during fasting
- BHB as HDAC inhibitor and anti-inflammatory agent
- BDNF increases with fasting (primarily animal studies; limited human data)
- Ketogenic diet maintaining cognitive function
- Neuroprotective properties of ketone bodies

### 11.2 Supported but Less Robust (Moderate Scientific Support)

- "Keto-adaptation" period of 2-4 days
- Improved mental clarity during established ketosis
- Temporal pattern of brain fog followed by clarity
- Enhanced focus related to norepinephrine elevation

### 11.3 Primarily Anecdotal (Limited Scientific Support)

- Enhanced creativity during fasting
- Sustained emotional stability
- Euphoria and heightened awareness
- Specific day-by-day cognitive timeline (individual variation is substantial)

---

## 12. Conclusion

The mental and cognitive journey during extended water fasting reflects underlying metabolic and neurochemical changes that proceed through predictable phases. The initial 24-48 hours typically involve cognitive challenges as glucose availability declines before ketone production compensates. Days 2-4 represent a transition period as the brain upregulates ketone utilization machinery. From day 4 onwards, established ketosis provides efficient fuel to the brain, and many individuals report enhanced mental clarity.

The neurobiological mechanisms supporting this cognitive journey include: ketone bodies serving as efficient alternative fuel; norepinephrine elevation enhancing arousal and attention; BDNF upregulation supporting synaptic plasticity; and anti-inflammatory effects of BHB improving brain network function.

However, the magnitude of cognitive benefits during fasting should be interpreted cautiously. While ketosis maintains adequate brain function and may offer modest enhancements in certain cognitive domains, the more dramatic experiences of "keto clarity" and euphoria reported anecdotally may exceed what controlled studies support. Individual variability is substantial, and factors including expectations, previous experience, and baseline metabolic health influence subjective experience.

Extended water fasting induces a fascinating natural experiment in brain metabolism, one that our ancestors experienced regularly throughout human evolution. The cognitive adaptations during fasting likely represent evolutionarily conserved mechanisms that maintained mental function during periods of food scarcity. Understanding these mechanisms provides insight into both human physiology and the potential therapeutic applications of ketogenic interventions.
