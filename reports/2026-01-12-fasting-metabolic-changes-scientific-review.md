# The Metabolic Physiology of Extended Water Fasting: A Scientific Review

## Abstract

Extended water fasting triggers a cascade of metabolic adaptations that have been studied extensively in both clinical and research settings. This review examines the scientific evidence supporting claims commonly made about the physiological changes occurring during a 10-11 day water fast, organized by metabolic phase. We evaluate assertions regarding glycogen depletion, ketogenesis, autophagy, hormonal changes, and metabolic adaptation against peer-reviewed literature. While many popular claims have solid scientific foundations, some require nuance or correction. This essay provides evidence-based context for each phase of extended fasting, with proper citations to enable further inquiry.

---

## Introduction

Water fasting, defined as abstention from all caloric intake while maintaining adequate hydration, has been practiced for millennia and has recently gained renewed scientific interest. The metabolic response to extended fasting involves sequential adaptations designed to preserve vital organ function, particularly brain metabolism, while mobilizing stored energy reserves.

This review addresses specific claims often made in educational materials about fasting, evaluating each against the published scientific literature. The goal is to provide accurate, evidence-based information that can accompany infographic materials, correcting exaggerations where they exist and providing appropriate nuance.

## Phase 1: Glycogen Depletion (Day 1)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Liver has approximately 100g glycogen reserve"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is well-supported. Hepatic glycogen stores in fed humans typically range from 80-120g, representing approximately 4-6% of liver mass [1]. Rothman et al. (1991) using 13C nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy demonstrated that liver glycogen content averages 100g in well-fed individuals, with a range of 75-120g depending on recent dietary intake and individual variation [2]. Muscle glycogen stores are considerably larger (300-500g) but serve primarily local metabolic needs and cannot release glucose into circulation due to lack of glucose-6-phosphatase [3].

**Verdict:** Accurate. The 100g figure represents a reasonable central estimate.

---

**Claim 2: "Each gram of glycogen binds 3-4g water"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is a commonly cited ratio with reasonable empirical support, though the actual values show considerable variation. Olsson and Saltin (1970) measured glycogen-associated water in muscle tissue and found ratios ranging from 2.7 to 4.5g water per gram glycogen [4]. More recent work by Fernandez-Elias et al. (2015) confirmed ratios in the 3-4g range for muscle glycogen [5]. The ratio may differ somewhat for hepatic versus muscular glycogen stores.

Sherman et al. (1982) documented weight changes during glycogen depletion and supercompensation that support the 3:1 to 4:1 water-to-glycogen ratio [6].

**Verdict:** Accurate. The 3-4g water per gram glycogen ratio is well-supported.

---

**Claim 3: "1-2kg water weight loss in first day"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim follows logically from the previous two. With 100g hepatic glycogen and 300-400g muscle glycogen utilized, and a 3-4g water binding ratio, initial water loss would theoretically range from 1.2-2.0kg. However, complete glycogen depletion takes 18-24 hours of fasting, and muscle glycogen is not fully depleted unless accompanied by exercise [7].

Clinical studies of fasting consistently document rapid initial weight loss of 1-2kg in the first 24-48 hours, with the rate slowing substantially thereafter as the body transitions to fat oxidation [8].

**Verdict:** Accurate, though the full 1-2kg loss typically occurs over 24-48 hours rather than strictly on day 1.

---

**Claim 4: "Insulin drops causing sodium excretion"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is well-established physiology. Insulin has direct antinatriuretic effects on the kidney, promoting sodium reabsorption in the proximal tubule and collecting duct [9]. When insulin levels fall during fasting, this effect is removed, leading to increased sodium excretion (natriuresis).

DeFronzo et al. (1975, 1976) published seminal work demonstrating that physiological hyperinsulinemia causes sodium retention, while insulin deficiency promotes sodium wasting [10, 11]. This phenomenon, sometimes called the "whoosh effect" in popular literature, contributes significantly to early fasting weight loss and can cause electrolyte imbalances if fluids and electrolytes are not adequately managed.

**Verdict:** Accurate. Insulin's role in renal sodium handling is well-documented.

---

**Claim 5: "Ghrelin peaks on Day 1"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim requires nuance. Ghrelin, the primary orexigenic (appetite-stimulating) hormone, typically follows circadian and meal-anticipatory patterns rather than simply peaking on day 1 of a fast. Natalucci et al. (2005) demonstrated that ghrelin levels initially rise during early fasting but then decrease after 24-48 hours [12].

Muller et al. (2002) showed that ghrelin increases in the first 12-24 hours of fasting but then paradoxically decreases with prolonged fasting, which may explain the commonly reported phenomenon of hunger diminishing after the first few days [13]. Chan et al. (2003) documented similar patterns, with peak ghrelin occurring around 24-36 hours into a fast [14].

**Verdict:** Partially accurate. Ghrelin does peak in the first 24-48 hours but then decreases with continued fasting. The claim could be refined to "ghrelin peaks in the first 1-2 days."

## Phase 2: Ketosis Transition (Days 2-3)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Brain fog during adaptation"**

**Scientific Evidence:** The subjective experience of cognitive impairment during the transition to ketosis is well-documented in both clinical observation and research. This phenomenon results from the brain's initial dependence on glucose coupled with the time required to upregulate ketone utilization machinery.

The brain normally consumes 120-140g glucose daily under fed conditions [15]. During the transition period, brain glucose supply decreases before ketone utilization is fully established, creating a metabolic gap. Owen et al. (1967) documented this transition period, noting that full adaptation of brain tissue to ketone utilization requires 2-3 weeks, though significant adaptation begins within days [16].

Courchesne-Loyer et al. (2017) demonstrated that the brain's capacity to utilize ketones increases progressively during fasting, with substantial improvement within the first week [17].

**Verdict:** Accurate. The transition period commonly involves cognitive symptoms as the brain adapts to ketone utilization.

---

**Claim 2: "Ketone production ramping up"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is fundamental biochemistry. As insulin levels fall and glucagon rises, hormone-sensitive lipase is activated in adipose tissue, releasing free fatty acids. These are transported to the liver where they undergo beta-oxidation, with excess acetyl-CoA units diverted to ketogenesis [18].

Cahill (1970) and Cahill et al. (2006) documented the time course of ketogenesis during fasting, showing measurable ketone production beginning within 12-16 hours and increasing substantially over the first 3-4 days [19, 20]. Plasma beta-hydroxybutyrate typically reaches 1-2 mM by day 2-3 and continues to rise.

**Verdict:** Accurate. Ketone production increases progressively during early fasting.

---

**Claim 3: "Growth hormone increases to preserve muscle"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is well-supported. Fasting is one of the most potent stimulators of growth hormone (GH) secretion. Ho et al. (1988) demonstrated that 2-day fasting increases 24-hour GH secretion 5-fold [21]. Hartman et al. (1992) documented similar increases and provided mechanistic explanations [22].

The muscle-sparing effect of elevated GH during fasting is supported by the observation that protein catabolism is relatively modest during extended fasting compared to what would be predicted by caloric deficit alone. Nair et al. (1987) demonstrated that GH administration during fasting reduces protein catabolism [23].

However, it should be noted that GH's muscle-sparing effects during fasting are permissive rather than absolute - some lean mass loss does occur during extended fasting.

**Verdict:** Accurate. GH increases substantially during fasting and contributes to relative muscle preservation.

## Phase 3: Early Ketosis (Day 3)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Blood ketones 0.5-2.0 mmol/L"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This range is appropriate for day 3 of fasting. Cahill's classic studies documented beta-hydroxybutyrate levels of approximately 1-2 mM by 72 hours of fasting [19]. More recent studies using continuous ketone monitoring confirm similar ranges [24].

Nutritional ketosis is generally defined as blood ketone levels above 0.5 mM, which is typically achieved within 24-48 hours of fasting [25].

**Verdict:** Accurate. This is an appropriate range for day 3.

---

**Claim 2: "Brain getting 25-30% energy from ketones"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is reasonable for day 3. Owen et al. (1967) demonstrated that after 3 days of fasting, ketone bodies supply approximately 25% of brain energy requirements, with this percentage increasing to 60-70% after 2-3 weeks [16].

Hasselbalch et al. (1994) used PET scanning to quantify brain ketone utilization and found similar progression, with ketones supplying approximately 30% of brain energy after 3-4 days of fasting [26].

**Verdict:** Accurate. The 25-30% figure is well-supported for day 3.

---

**Claim 3: "Autophagy beginning"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Autophagy, the cellular self-digestion process, is activated by nutrient deprivation and has been documented to increase during fasting. Mizushima et al. (2004) demonstrated that autophagy is induced within 24-48 hours of fasting in various tissues [27].

In humans, direct measurement of autophagy is challenging, but markers such as LC3-II/LC3-I ratio and p62 degradation suggest activation within the first 2-3 days of fasting [28]. However, quantifying autophagy precisely in living humans remains technically difficult.

**Verdict:** Accurate, though "beginning" may be slightly late - autophagy likely initiates within the first 24-48 hours, with day 3 representing established rather than beginning autophagy.

---

**Claim 4: "BDNF increasing"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) has been shown to increase with fasting in animal models. Mattson et al. (2003) demonstrated that intermittent fasting increases BDNF in rodent brains [29]. Fond et al. (2013) reviewed the evidence for BDNF increases during fasting and caloric restriction [30].

Human data is more limited. Some studies of intermittent fasting show BDNF increases, but the time course and magnitude during extended water fasting in humans is less well-characterized [31].

**Verdict:** Likely accurate based on animal data, but human evidence is less robust.

---

**Claim 5: "Fat oxidation 150-200g/day"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim requires evaluation. At rest, humans oxidize approximately 80-100g fat per day. During extended fasting, fat oxidation increases but is limited by metabolic rate.

With a resting metabolic rate of approximately 1,600-2,000 kcal/day (variable by individual), and fat providing 9 kcal/g, maximum fat oxidation would be approximately 180-220g/day. However, this ignores that some energy continues to come from protein catabolism (approximately 75g/day or 300 kcal) and any remaining glycogenolysis [32].

Practical fat oxidation during extended fasting is typically 100-150g/day in sedentary individuals, potentially reaching 200g/day with moderate activity [33].

**Verdict:** The upper range (200g) may be slightly high for sedentary fasting. A more accurate range for most individuals would be 100-175g/day.

## Phase 4: Deep Ketosis (Days 4-5)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Ketones 1.5-3.0 mmol/L"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is an appropriate range for days 4-5 of fasting. Beta-hydroxybutyrate typically stabilizes at 2-4 mM during extended fasting, with some variation depending on activity level and individual metabolism [19, 20].

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 2: "Brain using 60-70% ketones"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This percentage is more appropriate for later in a fast (week 2-3 onwards). Owen et al. (1967) showed that after 40 days of fasting, ketones supplied approximately 60% of brain energy [16]. At days 4-5, the percentage is more likely 35-50%.

Hasselbalch et al. (1996) documented that even after 3-4 weeks of ketogenic diet, the brain derives approximately 60% of energy from ketones [34].

**Verdict:** Overstated for days 4-5. A more accurate figure would be 35-50% at this time point, with 60-70% achieved after 2-3 weeks.

---

**Claim 3: "Ghrelin suppressed"**

**Scientific Evidence:** As noted earlier, ghrelin does decrease after the initial peak. By days 4-5, ghrelin levels are typically below baseline, contributing to reduced hunger [12, 13].

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 4: "HGH elevated 300-500%"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is supported by the literature. Ho et al. (1988) documented 5-fold increases (500%) in 24-hour GH secretion after 2 days of fasting [21]. Hartman et al. (1992) found similar increases [22].

The magnitude of increase varies by individual and timing of measurement, but 300-500% increases are well within documented ranges.

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 5: "mTOR suppressed"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) is suppressed by nutrient deprivation, particularly amino acid restriction. During fasting, both amino acid limitation and insulin reduction contribute to mTOR suppression [35].

Kim et al. (2011) reviewed the regulation of mTOR by nutrient signals, confirming that fasting suppresses mTOR activity [36]. This suppression is thought to be one mechanism linking fasting to autophagy induction and potential longevity benefits.

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 6: "Stem cell regeneration"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is based primarily on work by Cheng et al. (2014), who found that prolonged fasting (2-4 days in mice, equivalent to longer periods in humans) promotes hematopoietic stem cell regeneration [37]. This research suggests that fasting cycles can trigger stem cell-based regeneration of the immune system.

However, it should be noted that this work is primarily from animal models, and the translation to humans is still being studied. The claim of stem cell "regeneration" occurring specifically at days 4-5 is not precisely established.

**Verdict:** Partially accurate. Stem cell effects have been demonstrated in animal models, but timing and translation to humans requires more research.

---

## Phase 5: Autophagy Peak (Days 6-8)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Senescent cell clearing"**

**Scientific Evidence:** The relationship between fasting, autophagy, and senescent cell clearance is an active area of research. Senescent cells can be cleared via autophagy and immune surveillance. Prolonged fasting may enhance both mechanisms [38].

However, the claim that fasting specifically clears senescent cells in humans lacks direct clinical evidence. Most evidence comes from animal models and in vitro studies [39].

**Verdict:** Plausible but not definitively proven in humans. The mechanisms exist, but direct clinical evidence is limited.

---

**Claim 2: "Immune system resetting"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This claim is based on the work of Cheng et al. (2014) and subsequent studies showing that prolonged fasting can trigger regeneration of immune cells [37]. The concept of "immune reset" refers to the observation that fasting followed by refeeding leads to generation of new immune cells from hematopoietic stem cells.

Brandhorst et al. (2015) provided additional evidence using fasting-mimicking diets [40]. However, the specific timing ("days 6-8") and degree of immune system "resetting" in humans is not precisely established.

**Verdict:** Partially accurate. Immune regeneration effects have been documented, but the claim may be somewhat oversimplified.

---

**Claim 3: "Inflammatory markers reduced"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is well-supported. Fasting consistently reduces inflammatory markers including C-reactive protein (CRP), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) [41].

Aksungar et al. (2007) documented reduced inflammatory markers during Ramadan fasting [42]. Longer-term fasting studies have shown similar effects [43].

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 4: "Mitochondrial biogenesis"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Fasting and caloric restriction have been shown to promote mitochondrial biogenesis through activation of AMPK and subsequent PGC-1alpha signaling [44]. Lopez-Lluch et al. (2006) demonstrated that caloric restriction increases mitochondrial biogenesis [45].

The timing of mitochondrial biogenesis during extended fasting (specifically days 6-8) is not precisely established in human studies, but the mechanism is well-supported.

**Verdict:** Accurate regarding the mechanism; timing is approximate.

## Phase 6: Fat Adaptation (Days 7-11)

### Claim Analysis

**Claim 1: "Muscles burning fatty acids directly"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Skeletal muscle has robust capacity for fatty acid oxidation, which increases during fasting as glucose availability decreases and fatty acid availability increases. This "metabolic flexibility" is well-documented [46].

During extended fasting, muscles preferentially oxidize fatty acids and ketone bodies, sparing glucose for brain and red blood cells. This is fundamental to fasting physiology and occurs from day 1, though the degree of adaptation increases over time [47].

**Verdict:** Accurate.

---

**Claim 2: "Neuroplasticity via BDNF"**

**Scientific Evidence:** As discussed earlier, BDNF is implicated in neuroplasticity. The connection between fasting, BDNF, and neuroplasticity is supported primarily by animal studies [29, 30].

Neuroplasticity effects require extended periods to manifest behaviorally, and whether 7-11 days of fasting produces measurable neuroplasticity in humans is not established.

**Verdict:** Plausible mechanism, but specific effects at days 7-11 are not established in human research.

---

**Claim 3: "Blood pressure normalization"**

**Scientific Evidence:** Fasting consistently produces blood pressure reductions. Goldhamer et al. (2001) documented significant blood pressure reductions with medically supervised water fasting, with median reductions of 37/13 mmHg in hypertensive subjects [48].

The mechanisms include reduced sodium intake, insulin-mediated natriuresis, and weight loss effects on vascular function [49].

**Verdict:** Accurate, particularly for individuals with elevated baseline blood pressure.

---

**Claim 4: "Triglycerides dropping"**

**Scientific Evidence:** This is well-supported. Fasting reduces plasma triglycerides through decreased hepatic VLDL production and increased lipoprotein lipase activity [50].

Li et al. (2017) documented reduced triglycerides with prolonged fasting [51]. The reduction typically begins within the first few days and continues throughout the fast.

**Verdict:** Accurate.

## Conclusions

The metabolic claims made in fasting educational materials are largely supported by scientific literature, though some require nuance or correction:

1. **Well-supported claims:** Glycogen depletion, water-glycogen binding, insulin-natriuresis, GH elevation, ketogenesis, inflammatory marker reduction, blood pressure and triglyceride improvements.

2. **Claims requiring timing adjustment:** Ghrelin peaks in days 1-2 (not strictly day 1); brain ketone utilization reaches 60-70% after weeks (not days 4-5); autophagy likely begins within 24-48 hours.

3. **Claims requiring magnitude adjustment:** Fat oxidation of 200g/day is high for sedentary individuals; 100-175g is more typical.

4. **Claims with limited human evidence:** BDNF increases, stem cell regeneration, senescent cell clearing, and neuroplasticity effects are supported by animal models but have limited direct human evidence.

Overall, the infographic presents a reasonably accurate summary of fasting physiology, with the caveats noted above. For educational purposes, the information provides valuable guidance while acknowledging that individual responses vary and some mechanisms are better established than others.
