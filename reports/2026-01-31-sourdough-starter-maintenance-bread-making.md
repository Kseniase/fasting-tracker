## Introduction

Sourdough bread has been made for thousands of years, long before commercial yeast existed. What makes it special is that instead of using store-bought yeast packets, sourdough relies on wild yeast and friendly bacteria that naturally exist in flour and the air around us. This guide will teach you how to create and care for a sourdough starter, and how to bake your first loaf of bread.

Think of a sourdough starter as a pet that lives in your kitchen. It needs to be fed regularly, kept at a comfortable temperature, and in return, it will help you make delicious bread for years to come.

---

## What is a Sourdough Starter?

### Simple Explanation

A sourdough starter is a mixture of flour and water that has been colonized by wild yeast and beneficial bacteria. According to [The Perfect Loaf](https://www.theperfectloaf.com/7-easy-steps-making-incredible-sourdough-starter-scratch/), "a sourdough starter is a mixture of flour and water that hosts a stable blend of beneficial bacteria and wild yeasts."

**In even simpler terms:** Imagine flour and water sitting in a jar. Tiny living things (too small to see) that float in the air and live on the flour start eating the natural sugars in the flour. When they eat, they burp out gas (carbon dioxide) and acids. The gas makes bubbles, and the acids make the sour taste. When you add this bubbly mixture to bread dough, those bubbles make the bread rise.

### The Science Made Simple

According to [Summit Sourdough](https://www.summitsourdough.com/en-us/blogs/information-and-process-5/science-of-sourdough-fermentation), during fermentation, yeast and bacteria convert simple sugars in the flour into carbon dioxide (that rises your bread), ethanol, and acids like lactic acid and acetic acid. The acids give sourdough its characteristic tangy flavor.

**Key Point:** You are not "catching" wild yeast from the air so much as cultivating the yeast that already exists on the flour itself. As [The Pantry Mama](https://www.pantrymama.com/where-does-the-yeast-in-sourdough-starter-come-from/) explains, "The first microorganisms to make a home in your sourdough starter come from the flour itself."

## Creating a Starter from Scratch

### What You Need

- A clean glass jar (at least 1-quart capacity)
- Flour (whole wheat, rye, or all-purpose)
- Water (filtered or left out overnight to remove chlorine)
- A kitchen scale (highly recommended)
- A rubber band or tape to mark the jar

### Day-by-Day Instructions

The following schedule is synthesized from [King Arthur Baking](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/sourdough-starter-recipe), [The Clever Carrot](https://www.theclevercarrot.com/2019/03/beginner-sourdough-starter-recipe/), and [Milk and Pop](https://milkandpop.com/making-a-sourdough-starter/).

#### Day 1: The Beginning

| Ingredient | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| Whole wheat or rye flour | 60-100g |
| Water (room temperature) | 60-100g |

**Instructions:**
1. Add flour and water to your clean jar
2. Mix thoroughly until no dry flour remains
3. Cover loosely (cloth, paper towel, or loose lid)
4. Place in a warm spot (70-75 degrees Fahrenheit)
5. Wait 24 hours

**What to expect:** Probably nothing. The mixture will just sit there. That is normal.

#### Day 2: First Feeding

**Instructions:**
1. Look for any bubbles (some activity is good, none is also fine)
2. Discard half the mixture (about 60-100g)
3. Add fresh flour: 60-100g
4. Add fresh water: 60-100g
5. Mix well, cover, wait 24 hours

**Why discard?** According to [King Arthur Baking](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/sourdough-starter-recipe), "By day four, you would have sourdough starter overflowing from your bowl. Also, removing half ensures that the right amount of flour and water is feeding the growing colony of beneficial yeast."

#### Day 3: Continue Feeding

1. You may see some bubbles and smell a slightly sour or fruity aroma
2. Repeat the discard and feeding process from Day 2
3. Continue waiting 24 hours

**Note:** Days 3-4 often show slower activity. This is called a "false start" according to [The Clever Carrot](https://www.theclevercarrot.com/2019/03/beginner-sourdough-starter-recipe/) and is completely normal.

#### Days 4-5: Building Strength

1. Continue daily feedings (discard half, feed equal parts flour and water)
2. By end of Day 5, you should see the starter doubling in volume
3. The smell should be pleasantly sour

#### Days 6-7: Increasing Activity

1. Begin feeding twice daily (every 12 hours) if your kitchen is warm
2. The starter should be reliably doubling in size
3. Lots of bubbles should be visible throughout

#### When Is It Ready?

Your starter is ready to bake with when it shows these signs:
- Doubles in size within 4-8 hours after feeding
- Has many bubbles of different sizes throughout
- Smells pleasantly sour (like yogurt or mild vinegar)
- Has a fluffy, airy texture

**The Float Test:** Drop a small spoonful of starter into a cup of water. If it floats, it is ready. However, as [The Perfect Loaf](https://www.theperfectloaf.com/21-common-sourdough-starter-problems-with-solutions/) notes, "The float test is not a definitive test for whether a starter is healthy" - some starters made with rye flour may never float but still work perfectly.

### Timeline Expectations

| Kitchen Temperature | Time to Ready Starter |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| Warm (75-80F) | 7 days |
| Average (70-75F) | 7-10 days |
| Cool (65-70F) | 10-14 days |
| Cold (below 65F) | 2-3 weeks |

## Daily and Weekly Maintenance

Once your starter is established, you have two main options for keeping it alive: counter storage (for frequent bakers) or refrigerator storage (for occasional bakers).

### Option 1: Counter Storage (Bake Often)

**Best for:** People who bake several times per week

**Feeding Schedule:** Every 12-24 hours

According to [Farmhouse on Boone](https://www.farmhouseonboone.com/how-to-care-for-sourdough-starter/), "If you leave your starter out on the counter, it will need to be fed equal parts water and flour every 12-24 hours."

**Basic Feeding Ratio (1:1:1):**

| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Existing starter | 113g (about 1/2 cup) |
| Fresh flour | 113g |
| Fresh water | 113g |

**Steps:**
1. Discard all but 113g of starter
2. Add 113g flour
3. Add 113g water
4. Mix well
5. Cover loosely
6. Repeat every 12-24 hours

### Option 2: Refrigerator Storage (Bake Occasionally)

**Best for:** People who bake once a week or less

**Feeding Schedule:** Once per week (can go up to 2-3 weeks in a pinch)

According to [King Arthur Baking](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/feeding-and-maintaining-your-sourdough-starter-recipe), "For most home bakers, daily feeding is impractical; so you'll need to store your starter in the refrigerator and feed it once a week."

**Weekly Maintenance Steps:**
1. Remove starter from refrigerator
2. Let it warm up for 1-2 hours
3. Stir in any liquid on top (called "hooch")
4. Discard all but 113g
5. Feed with 113g flour and 113g water
6. Let sit at room temperature for 1-2 hours until slightly bubbly
7. Return to refrigerator

**Important:** According to [The Pantry Mama](https://www.pantrymama.com/how-to-store-sourdough-starter-in-the-fridge/), "It's best to store only a well-established sourdough starter in the fridge - that means a starter that's at least a month old."

### Preparing to Bake from the Refrigerator

When you want to bake, your refrigerated starter needs to wake up.

**Steps:**
1. Remove from fridge, let warm 1-2 hours
2. Feed as normal (discard and add flour/water)
3. Wait 8-12 hours until it doubles
4. Feed again if needed
5. Continue until it doubles within 6-8 hours (usually 2-3 feedings)

According to [King Arthur Baking](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/feeding-and-maintaining-your-sourdough-starter-recipe), "Continue feeding every 12 hours until you see it double or triple in volume within 6 to 8 hours. That means it's strong enough to leaven bread."

### Quick Reference: Feeding Ratios

| Ratio | Use Case |
|-------|----------|
| 1:1:1 | Building new starter, preparing to bake |
| 1:2:2 | Daily maintenance on counter |
| 1:4:4 or 1:5:5 | Longer between feedings, cooler temps |

According to [Brod & Taylor](https://brodandtaylor.com/blogs/recipes/feeding-ratios), "Many bakers generally use a 1:4:4 ratio. This ratio promotes a vigorous starter with extended feeding intervals, ideal for most home bakers."

## Signs of Health and Troubleshooting

### Signs of a Healthy Starter

A happy starter will show these characteristics:

**Appearance:**
- Lots of bubbles throughout (visible from the side of the jar)
- Doubles in size within 4-8 hours of feeding
- Slightly domed or rounded on top when at peak
- Honeycomb-like structure when you look inside

**Smell:**
- Pleasantly sour, like yogurt or mild vinegar
- Fresh and slightly sweet right after feeding
- More tangy as it gets hungry
- May smell like beer or apple cider

**Texture:**
- Like thick pancake batter
- Pourable but not watery
- Light and airy, not dense

### Common Problems and Solutions

#### Problem 1: Not Rising or Sluggish

**Cause:** Usually temperature-related

**Solution:** According to [The Perfect Loaf](https://www.theperfectloaf.com/21-common-sourdough-starter-problems-with-solutions/), "Most commonly, the issue has to do with temperature. If your sourdough starter is kept at a low temp, even 70 degrees Fahrenheit, it will slow fermentation activity."

| Temperature | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Below 65F | Very slow, may seem dead |
| 65-70F | Slow but working |
| 70-75F | Optimal activity |
| 75-80F | Fast activity |
| Above 80F | Too fast, may over-ferment |

**Fix:** Move to a warmer spot. Top of refrigerator, inside oven with light on (not heat), or use a proofing box.

#### Problem 2: Liquid Layer on Top (Hooch)

**Cause:** Starter is hungry and has exhausted its food supply

**Solution:** According to [King Arthur Baking](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2012/04/08/maintaining-your-sourdough-starter-food-water-and-time), "Hooch that forms on top of your starter between feedings indicates that the starter is hungry."

**Fix:**
- Pour off or stir in the liquid
- Feed immediately
- Consider feeding more frequently or using a higher ratio

#### Problem 3: Smells Like Nail Polish or Acetone

**Cause:** Over-fermented, producing excess acids

**Solution:** According to [The Clever Carrot](https://www.theclevercarrot.com/2018/03/troubleshooting-your-sourdough-starter/), "A smell like acetone or nail polish usually means your starter is hungry and producing excess acids."

**Fix:**
- Feed immediately
- Feed more frequently
- Move to a cooler spot to slow fermentation

#### Problem 4: Not Doubling, But Lots of Bubbles

**Cause:** Mixture may be too wet

**Solution:** The starter is healthy but the consistency prevents visible rise. Try reducing water slightly in your next feeding (use 90g water instead of 100g).

#### Problem 5: Strange Colors

| Color | Meaning | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Cream/beige | Healthy | Continue as normal |
| Gray liquid | Hooch, hungry | Stir in or pour off, feed |
| Pink, orange | Contamination | Discard and start over |
| Green, black, white fuzzy spots | Mold | Discard immediately |

According to [The Pantry Mama](https://www.pantrymama.com/troubleshooting-your-sourdough-starter/), "Typical signs of food spoilage and mold include pink, orange, or green colors, white fuzzy spots, or sometimes areas that are darker with white areas on top. If you see any of these signs, throw your starter away and create a new one."

#### Problem 6: Taking Forever to Get Started (More Than 2 Weeks)

**Possible causes and fixes:**
1. **Water has chlorine:** Let tap water sit out overnight before using
2. **Using bleached flour:** Switch to unbleached all-purpose or add some whole wheat
3. **Too cold:** Find a warmer spot
4. **Not enough whole grain:** Add 20-30% whole wheat or rye flour

## Basic Sourdough Bread Recipe

This recipe is synthesized from [Alexandra's Kitchen](https://alexandracooks.com/2017/10/24/artisan-sourdough-made-simple-sourdough-bread-demystified-a-beginners-guide-to-sourdough-baking/) and [The Clever Carrot](https://www.theclevercarrot.com/2014/01/sourdough-bread-a-beginners-guide/).

### Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Notes |
|------------|--------|-------|
| Active sourdough starter | 50-100g | Bubbly and recently fed |
| Water | 350-375g | Warm (about 80F) |
| Bread flour | 500g | Can use all-purpose |
| Salt | 9-10g | About 2 teaspoons |

### Equipment Needed

- Large mixing bowl
- Kitchen scale
- Dutch oven or heavy pot with lid
- Proofing basket or bowl lined with towel
- Bench scraper or large spatula
- Sharp knife or razor blade for scoring
- Parchment paper

### Timeline Overview

| Step | Time |
|------|------|
| Mix dough | 10 minutes |
| Rest (autolyse) | 30 minutes |
| Stretch and fold | 2 hours (4 sets) |
| Bulk fermentation | 3-10 hours |
| Shape | 10 minutes |
| Cold proof in fridge | 8-48 hours |
| Bake | 45-50 minutes |
| Cool | 1 hour minimum |
| **Total** | **About 24-36 hours** |

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### Step 1: Mix the Dough (Evening, Day 1)

1. Add 350g warm water to a large bowl
2. Add 50-100g active starter
3. Mix with a fork until the starter dissolves (water will look cloudy)
4. Add 500g flour and 10g salt
5. Mix with your hands until a shaggy dough forms (no dry flour visible)
6. Cover with a damp towel or plastic wrap
7. Let rest 30 minutes (this is called autolyse)

#### Step 2: Stretch and Fold (Build Strength)

According to [Alexandra's Kitchen](https://alexandracooks.com/2017/10/24/artisan-sourdough-made-simple-sourdough-bread-demystified-a-beginners-guide-to-sourdough-baking/), "After 30 minutes, grab a corner of the dough and pull it up and into the center. Repeat until you've performed this series of folds 4 to 5 times."

**How to do it:**
1. Wet your hand (prevents sticking)
2. Grab one side of the dough
3. Stretch it up and fold it over to the other side
4. Turn the bowl 90 degrees
5. Repeat stretching and folding
6. Do this 4 times (going all around the dough)
7. Cover and rest 30 minutes
8. Repeat the stretch and fold 3-4 more times over the next 2 hours

**Why do this?** Stretching and folding develops gluten (makes the bread chewy) and incorporates air (makes it light).

#### Step 3: Bulk Fermentation (The Big Rise)

After your last stretch and fold, cover the dough and let it rise.

**Time depends on temperature:**

| Dough Temperature | Expected Time |
|-------------------|---------------|
| 65-70F | 8-12 hours |
| 70-75F | 6-8 hours |
| 75-80F | 4-6 hours |

According to [The Sourdough Journey](https://thesourdoughjourney.com/faq-bulk-fermentation-timing/), "For most sourdough recipes, expect bulk fermentation to take 2 to 5 hours" at warm temperatures, but it can take much longer in cooler conditions.

**How to know it is done:**
- Dough has increased 50-75% in size
- Surface has some bubbles
- Dough jiggles when you move the bowl
- Edges pull away slightly from the bowl

#### Step 4: Shape the Dough

1. Flour your counter lightly
2. Gently turn dough out onto counter (do not punch down)
3. Using a bench scraper, fold the edges toward the center
4. Flip the dough over (seam side down)
5. Use your hands to gently rotate and tighten into a ball
6. Let rest 20-30 minutes (bench rest)
7. Shape again if needed for a tighter ball
8. Place seam-side up in a floured proofing basket or towel-lined bowl

#### Step 5: Cold Proof (Overnight, Into Day 2)

1. Cover the basket with plastic wrap or a shower cap
2. Place in refrigerator
3. Let proof 8-48 hours (overnight is perfect)

**Why cold proof?** According to [Alexandra's Kitchen](https://alexandracooks.com/2017/10/24/artisan-sourdough-made-simple-sourdough-bread-demystified-a-beginners-guide-to-sourdough-baking/), "Refrigerate for 1 hour or for as long as 48 hours (24+ hours recommended for best results)." Cold proofing develops flavor and makes the dough easier to handle and score.

#### Step 6: Bake (Day 2)

1. Place Dutch oven in oven
2. Preheat to 450-500F for at least 30 minutes
3. Remove dough from fridge (do not let it warm up)
4. Place parchment paper on counter
5. Turn dough out onto parchment (seam side down now)
6. Score the top with a sharp blade (make a 1/4 inch deep slash)
7. Carefully place dough (on parchment) into hot Dutch oven
8. Cover with lid
9. Bake 30 minutes covered
10. Remove lid
11. Bake 15-20 minutes more until deep golden brown
12. Internal temperature should reach 205-210F

#### Step 7: Cool and Enjoy

According to [The Clever Carrot](https://www.theclevercarrot.com/2014/01/sourdough-bread-a-beginners-guide/), "Cool on a wire rack minimum 1 hour before slicing - don't cut too soon or the inside will have a damp and gummy texture."

**Be patient!** The bread is still cooking inside as it cools. Cutting too early releases steam and results in gummy bread.

## Beginner Tips

### Top 10 Tips for Success

1. **Use a kitchen scale.** Measuring cups are inconsistent. Weight measurements ensure repeatable results.

2. **Temperature matters more than time.** A warm kitchen speeds everything up; a cold one slows things down. Adjust your expectations accordingly.

3. **Watch the dough, not the clock.** As [The Perfect Loaf](https://www.theperfectloaf.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-bread-dough-bulk-fermentation/) states, "Determining when to end bulk fermentation is one of the most challenging parts of making sourdough bread. It requires a holistic assessment of the dough."

4. **Start with small batches.** Until you are comfortable, make one loaf at a time.

5. **Keep notes.** Write down your timing, temperatures, and results. This helps you repeat successes and learn from mistakes.

6. **Do not add too much flour.** Sticky dough is normal. Wet hands work better than adding flour.

7. **Preheat your Dutch oven thoroughly.** A hot pot creates the steam needed for a good crust.

8. **Score confidently.** A hesitant score tears the dough. One swift, confident slash works best.

9. **Be patient during cooling.** The hardest part is waiting to cut into warm bread. Resist for at least an hour.

10. **Accept imperfection.** Even "failed" sourdough usually tastes delicious. The learning process is part of the joy.

### Troubleshooting Your First Loaves

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Dense, not rising | Starter not active enough | Feed starter more, wait for reliable doubling |
| Too sour | Over-fermented | Reduce bulk fermentation time |
| Not sour enough | Under-fermented | Longer cold proof (24-48 hours) |
| Gummy inside | Cut too soon OR under-baked | Wait longer to slice, check internal temp |
| Flat loaf | Over-proofed or weak shaping | Reduce proof time, practice shaping |
| Burned bottom | Dutch oven too hot | Place on lower rack, reduce temp |

## Conclusion

Creating and maintaining a sourdough starter is simpler than it might first appear. The basic process requires just flour, water, and patience. A starter can be created in 7-14 days by mixing flour and water daily, discarding half, and feeding with fresh ingredients. Once established, it can be maintained with weekly refrigerator feedings for occasional bakers or daily feedings for those who bake frequently.

The key indicators of a healthy starter are consistent: it should double in size within 4-8 hours of feeding, display many bubbles throughout, and have a pleasant sour smell. Most problems can be solved by adjusting temperature or feeding frequency.

Basic sourdough bread requires only four ingredients (starter, flour, water, salt) but does require time - typically 24-36 hours from mixing to eating. The process can be broken into manageable steps: mix in the evening, let rise overnight, shape in the morning, cold proof during the day, and bake that evening.

The most important advice for beginners is to be patient with both the starter and with yourself. Sourdough baking is a skill developed over time, and even imperfect loaves are usually delicious.
